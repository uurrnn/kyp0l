PROPERTY VALUATION ADMINISTRATION 

Project Name:   
Case Manager:  

Property Valuation Administration 
Thomas Hart, Director of Research and Valuation 

00:27:07 
Thomas  Hart  provided  an  overview  of  how  PVA  deals  with  conservation 
easements  in  Jefferson  County.  Hart  noted  that  his  department  is  informed  of  the 
conservation  easement  either  by  the  deed  or  the  owner  reaches  out  to  the  PVA 
department.  Hart  explained  that  as  part  of  the  valuation  process  PVA  looks  to  other 
properties  with  restrictions  on  development.  Hart  responded  to  questions  from  Board 
Members (see recording for details). 

00:35:52 
Jason Hancock, Chief Deputy, PVA mentioned that land with ten contiguous 
acres  qualifies  for  an  agricultural  exemption,  while  land  with  five  contiguous  acres 
qualifies for horticultural exemption. Hancock noted the land has to be actively farmed 
and the owner is required to get a farm number from the KY Department of Agriculture. 
PVA inspects these properties for the first 5 years and is open to consider appeals from 
property owners. Persons making appeals must provide supporting documentation, which 
may include appraisals. Mason commented that some conservation easement properties 
are  not  actively  farmed  but  are  managed  for  wildlife  and  other  purposes.  Hancock 
responded to questions from Board Members (see recording for details). 

An audio/visual recording of the Louisville/Jefferson County Environmental Trust 
Oversight Board meeting related to this case is available on the Office of Planning 
website, or you may contact the Customer Service staff to view the recording or to

LIMESTONE CLIFF FARM-UPDATE 

Project Name:   
Case Manager:  

Limestone Cliff Farm-Update 
Kurt Mason and Ann Scholtz, Assistant County Attorney 

00:54:13 
Kurt  Mason  stated  that  every  conservation  easement  that  comes  to  the 
Louisville/Jefferson County Environmental Trust has to go to Metro Council for approval. 
Mason noted that conservation easements are reviewed by the Oversight Board and help 
to work through the conservation easement deed document and then present LJCET’s 
recommendation to Metro Council to accept the easement. Limestone Cliff Farm came to 
LJCET  because  LJCET  holds  an  easement  on  existing  property  that  is  owned  by  the 
same owner. Mason mentioned the new proposed property is located in Oldham County 
and per the bylaws LJCET has the capability of evaluating properties that benefit LJCET. 
Proposed  amendments  to  the  conservation  easement  included  updating  the  address, 
reflecting  the  recent  name  change  of  the  Oldham  County  Comprehensive  Planning 
document,  and  the  addition  of  language  regarding  notification  when  structures  are 
developed  on  the  property  and  statements  consistent  with  the  effective  planning 
document.  Mason  responded  to  questions  from  Board  Members  (see  recording  for 
details).

METRO ORDINANCE OVERVIEW AND CONSERVATION EASEMENTS   

Project Name:   
Case Manager:  

Metro Ordinance Overview and Conservation Easements 
Kurt Mason and Ann Scholtz, Assistant County Attorney  

01:23:10

Boards mission statement and history. Scholtz noted that the Oversight Board shall set 
policies  for  the  Trust,  including  acquisition  and  stewardship  policies,  set  criteria  for 
selecting  projects,  provide  technical  expertise,  oversee  implementation  of  the  policies. 
Scholtz  also  noted  that  the  Oversight  Board  reviews  each  potential  conservation 
easement and votes to recommend, accept, or reject. Scholtz explained LJCET’s model 
conservation easement document (see recording for details).
